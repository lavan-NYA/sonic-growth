Phase 2 — Research Secondary Sources & Case Studies
Phytoacoustics
Sound is biology.
Sound waves create micro-vibrations in plant cell walls. At 100–500 Hz — the range of most music — these vibrations accelerate metabolic activity and cell division. South Korean RDA (2007) found rice exposed to specific sound frequencies showed up to 15% faster growth.

At high-frequency noise above 80 dB — the average Delhi street — plant stress responses activate: stomata close, CO₂ absorption drops, photosynthesis slows. The plant does not die from the noise. It closes itself off from the world.
Existing Solutions — What's Missing
No tool for everyday people.
PlantWave converts plant bioelectrical signals into music. Direction: plant → human. Not our problem to solve.

IKEA #BullyAPlant (2018) proved plants react to sonic/emotional environment. Viral, but no data tool followed.

Harvestgeist mapped plant responses to urban data — academic project, never public-facing.

Gap identified: No accessible, public tool for logging sound environment alongside plant health and watching the correlation emerge in real time.

Phase 3 — Observational Parameters What We Measured & Why
🔊
Ambient Sound Level
dB · NIOSH SLM app · Core independent variable. Measured at both locations twice daily — morning + evening.
🌿
Leaf Droop / Health
Ordinal 0–3 · Visual observation · 0 = healthy turgid leaves, 3 = severe droop + yellowing. Most visible stress marker.
😌
Personal Mood / Focus
Scale 1–5 · Self-log · One number, end of day. Tests the parallel human–plant wellbeing hypothesis in the same environment.
☀️
Light Level (approx.)
Lux estimate · Lux meter phone app · Controls for sunlight as a confounding variable between balcony and indoor plants.

Phase 3 — Design Thinking Data-to-Visual Mapping Table
Data Variable Data Type Visual Encoding Principle
Leaf Droop Score (0–3) Ordinal Colour gradient: green → amber → red across table rows Pre-attentive processing — colour hue registers before conscious attention
Ambient dB Level Quantitative Line chart with area fill — Y axis, X = days Cleveland & McGill: position on common scale = most accurate quantitative comparison
Light Level (lux) Quantitative Secondary Y-axis as dotted line overlay on dB chart Tufte data-ink ratio — overlay preserves space, shows relationship without extra panel
Personal Mood (1–5) Ordinal Small glyphs below timeline — circle fill level 1–5 Gestalt proximity — mood sits at the day it occurred; fills intuitively convey scale
Plant identity (A vs B) Categorical Consistent colour coding: sage = indoor B, rust = outdoor A Pre-attentive: colour hue distinguishes categories instantly across all panels
Day / Date Temporal X-axis (left → right), labelled with date Convention: time flows left to right — zero cognitive load

The Hook
Your plant is listening.
Urban residents invest in indoor plants for wellness, calm, air quality. They water on schedule, give them sunlight, repot when needed. But nobody asks: what is the plant hearing?

In a country where 74% of cities exceed WHO noise limits, and indoor plant ownership has surged post-2020, the acoustic environment is shaping plant health daily — invisibly. This project makes that visible.
The same sonic environment stressing your plant is stressing you. Biology is not separate from the built environment.
